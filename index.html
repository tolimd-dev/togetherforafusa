The Story

I'm currently stuck in the Istanbul airport because of a small but growing Ebola outbreak in Uganda, where I recently was. While here, my group and I met Afusa — a lovely woman from Kampala who was on her way to St. Louis University to start her Master's in Health Data Science.

She has been planning to do this program for a year now and her course was set to start on May 19th. Her American visa was very difficult to obtain, delaying her ability to get to the states early — and now it appears that she may not make it in time. She is thinking about returning to Uganda and perhaps trying to start the program again in the fall, instead of in the next several days.

This is her first time going to the states. She's put in the work to get this Master's, done all of the right things, and her ability to start the program is being compromised.

Does anyone have contacts at St. Louis University so that we can help support her? Off the cuff — allowing her to start virtually and then come in person later would be an awesome option. We may just need to vouch for her.

Updates
May 19, 2026  ·  4:30 AM CST Latest

The CDC has now issued a formal 30-day travel ban for travelers in Afusa's situation — see the Politico article on the new CDC travel restrictions.

However, Afusa's case seems to fit outside the norm: she was already almost in the states when she was stopped, and her actual exposure to Ebola and likelihood of carrying it are extremely low.

Here's where things stand and what's being explored:

  • SLU is actively working on this. I've reached out to them with a few additional thoughts.
  • Is there a way for her to receive an exception from the department issuing travel restrictions?
  • Could she stay at a remote SLU site in Turkey (or somewhere else) for the mandated 30-day waiting period?
  • Turkish Airlines is telling Afusa she needs to BUY a ticket back to Uganda — even though they removed her from their flight and she already paid for her original ticket to the U.S. (which it sounds like she's not being refunded). If she has to fly back, there is absolutely no way she should have to pay for that ticket.

Stay tuned. Your help and support mean so much!

May 18, 2026  ·  2:30 PM CST

Thank you immensely to everyone who helped out. We're getting somewhere.

Afusa has received tons of support via phone calls, WhatsApp messages, and emails — including from DPC doctors with similar backgrounds and people who live or work in the region. The school faculty and staff are also working to find a way in. It appears there are some U.S. airports she may be able to fly into, and they're working on that.

I'm no longer in Istanbul, but from what I understand, she has what she needs and is taking everything in stride.

A bright spot: In the 2–3 hours we spent together at the airport, we "vibe-coded" a prototype of an idea of hers — a tool to help Uganda track and manage malaria cases. Check out the malaria tracker prototype → (may take a moment to load)
